Who we are

A Land for All (formerly Two States, One Homeland) is an Israeli–Palestinian movement that proposes a two-state confederation as the path out of the conflict. It brings together Israeli Jews, Palestinian citizens of Israel, and Palestinians around a shared premise: that the two peoples are not going anywhere, that neither will give up its claim to the whole land, and that the future therefore has to be built on partnership rather than separation alone.

The vision

Two sovereign, independent states — Israel and Palestine, along the 1967 lines — linked in a confederation. Each state is self-governing, but an open border allows freedom of movement and residence: people could live across the homeland while voting where they hold citizenship. Jerusalem would be an open, shared capital of both states. Shared institutions would manage the questions that cross every border — security, water, climate, human rights, and the economy. The model is grounded in equality, dignity, interdependence, and security for both peoples.

Shared institutions

Leadership

ALFA is led by co-directors May Pundak, an Israeli lawyer and activist, and Dr. Rula Hardal, a Palestinian citizen of Israel and political scientist — a genuinely shared, bi-national leadership that mirrors the movement's premise.

Strategy Retreat · Day Two

Sharpening the International Strategy

Two days in which ALFA's staff, board, and assembly members — joined by colleagues from the International Peace Institute (IPI) — worked to focus the movement's international engagement and connect it to change on the ground.

Why we gathered

Over the past two years, ALFA's international work has expanded dramatically. The retreat's purpose was to move from whether and where the movement engages internationally to how — how to ensure that diplomatic engagement actually advances ALFA's goals and contributes to political change at home. The day brought board and assembly members into a conversation that ALFA staff and two colleagues from the International Peace Institute (IPI) — Emmanuel and Arthur — had begun the day before.

Session 2 · International Engagement Review

Taking Stock of Two Years on the Global Stage

A candid review of what ALFA has learned from two years of international policy engagement — what is working, where the harder questions now lie, and how global reach can translate into change at home.

ALFA has moved from being introduced to being asked tougher questions.

The task now is to match a compelling vision with the policy depth, the messaging discipline, and the local roots that turn recognition into influence.

Key themes

1From introductions to asks›

For two years the goal was largely to be known — to get ALFA and its vision into the room. The movement has succeeded: most relevant international actors now know ALFA and are beginning to understand its vision, and increasingly treat it as a resource. That success creates a new task. The question is no longer just being seen; it is being clear about what ALFA actually wants from each actor — and, just as importantly, what it can offer them.

2The credibility test›

With recognition come harder questions. Interlocutors want more than a vision; they want policy depth — concrete answers on sequencing, security, mobility, and "what now," especially against the backdrop of Gaza and the West Bank. Meeting that demand means investing in research and policy, and widening the bench of people who can speak for the movement so that audiences hear fresh voices and new angles, not the same messenger each time.

3The messaging tension›

Diplomats expect, and cling to, the language of two states; anything that sounds like it threatens that framing makes them nervous. ALFA's fuller vision — a confederation, one shared homeland, freedom of movement — has to be conveyed without losing those audiences. The working answer that emerged: hold the two-state frame as the door, and the confederation as the room behind it — "two states, but adaptive" — tailoring emphasis to each audience without misrepresenting the vision.

4What ALFA uniquely offers›

ALFA's distinctive value is not "another plan." It is a whole worldview — a set of values, norms, and practices, and a recognition that an agreement is only the beginning. The central lesson of Oslo was that everything happened at the level of leaders, leaving a gap between the framework and the societies. ALFA's answer is to build the practices of partnership now — joint projects, shared institutions, "shadow" structures and assemblies — so that the vision is embodied, not just signed.

From global reach to local impact

The hardest question of the session: how to translate international progress into change at home. Participants framed impact on three levels, and noted that engagement abroad only matters if it moves these levels at home.

Points raised in discussion

Internal
"Two states" as a misnomer — and "rescue"›

Participants argued the heart of the matter is Palestinian independence — an asymmetry between the peoples, not a symmetry — and framed the confederation as a rescue of both a Palestinian and a Jewish future, language that aligns with international law.

Talk to confederations›

Federal and confederal precedents — Switzerland, Canada, Belgium, the EU, the UK — are natural reference points and potential allies for the model; "two states, one homeland / ALFA" works as a memorable hook.

Movement is possible›

J Street's drift away from a rigid two-state framing toward new articulations — partly credited to years of ALFA engagement — was cited as proof the conversation can shift.

An under-used constituency›

Palestinian citizens of Israel ('48 Palestinians) were seen as natural champions of the confederation agenda and an under-invested base.

The sanctions debate›

Whether ALFA should publicly back sanctions or accountability measures on Israel keeps surfacing in international forums (e.g., Paris). It was debated sharply, with a strong line of argument that taking it on would distract from — and dilute — ALFA's distinctive offer.

Tailoring the offer›

Shaping the "ask/offer" per audience — Gulf and regional actors (integration & security), European and US progressive politics (narrative), diplomats (policy depth) — without misrepresenting the vision.

Key takeaways

  • Be clear about the ask and the offer in every engagement.
  • Invest in policy depth and a wider bench of spokespeople.
  • Hold "two states" as the frame; carry the confederation within it.
  • An agreement is a beginning — build the practices now.
  • Measure success by impact at home, on all three levels.

Session 3 · Strategic Planning

Planning the Year Ahead

Turning reflection into a plan — mapping the year's international moments, choosing where to invest, and naming the priorities that have to be ready in time.

A calendar of opportunities

The year ahead offers a sequence of international moments. The UN General Assembly in New York (September) is the anchor. Around it: the Paris Peace Forum, the Munich Security Conference, the Doha Forum, the BRICS summit, and the Global Alliance process on implementing the two-state solution — alongside elections in Israel and the US midterms. On the horizon: the 80th anniversary of the UN Partition resolution (29 November 2027) as a longer-term mobilization point.

A way to prioritize

ALFA is a small organization; it cannot be the star of every event. For each opportunity the movement asks four questions:

1Why is this an opportunity for us?
2What does it require of us?
3What do we get?
4What do we give?
The discipline is to invest where effort compounds — where preparing for one moment also serves the next — and to start with one (the UNGA) rather than spread thin.

Three arenas of engagement

High-level
Ministerial settings — the Middle East dinner, Munich, Davos
Working-level
Closed-door, expert, Chatham-House-style discussions, often with partners
Bilateral
One-to-one meetings — in New York and on the road

Getting ready — the "how"

What it takes to be ready for the moments›
  • A tracking mechanism so engagements build year to year and no one "goes cold" — even when the person representing ALFA changes.
  • Audience-specific preparation — know who is in the room, what resonated last time, and which one or two points to lead with.
  • Coordination with other Israeli–Palestinian actors attending the same forums, so ALFA's vision gets named on panels.
  • A wider bench of speakers, trained through an apprenticeship model (an experienced voice mentoring a newer one).
  • Better documentation — capturing interventions on video and in writing so the work can be shown and reused.
  • Research into policy — turning the movement's research into short, digestible memos that member states can actually use.

Priorities toward the UNGA

Focus

Four priorities converged as the focus for the months before the General Assembly:

Name the regional moment

The conflict has become a regional one; ALFA can speak to integration, stability, and security as the frame that also addresses the Palestinian question — and to its urgency.

"No return to the status quo"

Around the elections, press the case that a new government must not slide back to merely managing the conflict — and that a legitimate, elected partner is needed on both sides.

Transformative recognition

Recognition of a Palestinian state is a beginning, not an end; the priority is to articulate what comes after recognition — the steps, relations, and obligations that follow.

Anniversaries as anchors

Recent diplomatic plans and, further out, the 2027 Partition anniversary offer natural moments to reframe the conversation.

Points raised in discussion

Internal
The 80th anniversary of UN Resolution 181›

The Partition Plan — "two states and an economic union," essentially ALFA's plan — marks its 80th anniversary on 29 November 2027, offering an 18-month mobilization horizon for civil society, the Arab League, and the EU.

Transformative recognition of Palestine›

Recognition as a beginning; position papers on what follows recognition — diplomatic and economic relations, obligations, and liberation from occupation.

The Trump-plan one-year anniversary›

A hook to comment on the impasse and propose a direction.

Munich — "alternatives to security"›

Orchestrate a panel on security beyond walls and checkpoints, where ALFA actually has something to say.

Palestinian elections›

Demand free elections in Palestine (and Israel) so there is a legitimate, elected partner; encourage ALFA-aligned figures — especially Palestinian citizens of Israel — to participate.

Keep civil society in the room›

Press international actors to keep engaging civil society even when they can't engage a given government.

Research → policy›

With IPI, turn the working-group research (security, economy, and more) into short memos digestible to member states.

Scenarios to watch›

Two developments could reshape everything in the coming months — continued regional escalation (Lebanon, Gaza) and the outcome of elections. The plan has to be robust to a landscape that may look very different within weeks.

Working better together. A recurring reflection: replace last-minute scrambling with longer-term, collective planning, and keep the international and domestic strands connected — in partnership with IPI. The aim is to institutionalize this kind of strategic session, not treat it as a one-off.

What We Heard

Cross-Cutting Conclusions

Five threads ran through both sessions.

1

The vision needs a credible "how."

A compelling destination is not enough; ALFA must show the path — a sequence and a theory of change, beginning with ending the occupation and recognition.

2

Two states as the door, confederation as the room.

Adaptive messaging that meets audiences where they are without diluting the vision.

3

Global engagement only matters if it changes things at home.

Impact runs through political elites, grass-tops, and grassroots — in both societies.

4

Protect what is distinctive.

ALFA is the rare cross-border, bi-national political movement with a positive vision; that distinctiveness should not be traded away by absorbing other agendas.

5

Capacity and process are strategy.

A dedicated international function, a tracking system, a wider bench of voices, better documentation, and longer-term planning are not "admin" — they are how the vision travels.

The movement's edge is not a better slogan; it is a different way of thinking about the conflict — and the discipline to make it concrete.
Forward Agenda

The Road Ahead

Six workstreams carry the retreat's thinking into the year — sequenced, with the UN General Assembly first.

1

Sharpen the "how."

Develop the theory of change and sequencing — ending the occupation and recognition as the first step toward the confederation vision.

2

Build the international function.

A clear strategy, dedicated capacity, and a tracking mechanism for engagements.

3

Deepen policy depth.

Position papers and short memos on security, recognition, and regional integration.

4

Widen the bench & tell the story.

More trained spokespeople; stronger documentation and communications.

5

Anchor globally, act locally.

Translate engagement into impact across elites, grass-tops, and grassroots.

6

Prepare for the moments.

UNGA first; then Paris, Munich, and Doha; with 2027 on the horizon.
